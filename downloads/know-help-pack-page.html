What this is

Not a persona.
A point of view.

A Clone pack is different from a system prompt or a Claude Project. It's a structured knowledge base of how a specific person thinks — their taste filters, decision criteria, red lines, and the references they trust. Claude doesn't roleplay this person. Claude reasons from their actual knowledge.

When a founder works on their brand and asks "is this logo working?" — Claude doesn't say "a good designer might consider..." It evaluates against the actual taste filters in this pack: does it own something, does it work in one color, does it survive at 16px, does it feel like the company or just like design.

The files load contextually. Logo work loads the logo and taste filter files. Type selection loads the typography file. Color decisions load the color file. Not everything at once — only what's relevant.

The 6 Taste Filters

Applied in order. Most work fails at filter 1 or 2. Getting to filter 5 means you have something worth refining.

  • Filter 1
    Does it own something?
    Can this be mistaken for a competitor? If yes, stop. Refinement won't fix it. What does it own that nothing else in this category owns?
  • Filter 2
    Does it work without color?
    Show it in black and white. If it falls apart, the mark itself isn't strong enough. Color is a modifier, not a structural element.
  • Filter 3
    Does it survive scale?
    Show it at 16px × 16px and at 2000px wide. Both must work. The 16px test kills intricate marks. The 2000px test kills marks that hide weaknesses at small scale.
  • Filter 4
    Would a layperson recognize it as intentional?
    Not "does a layperson like it?" — whether it reads as a considered choice, not an accident. If it doesn't read as intentional to a non-designer, the communication has failed.
  • Filter 5
    Can the company actually use this?
    PowerPoint slide background. Email footer. Embroidered on a hat. Printed in one color on a coffee cup. Dark AND light backgrounds. All of these.
  • Filter 6
    Does it feel like the company, or like design?
    Work that feels like design — that looks like a designer made something — is often not the right answer. The goal is work that feels like the company.

Logo Red Lines

These are not preferences. No amount of execution quality redeems a direction that crosses these.

  • Drop shadows on marks. A shadow signals the mark cannot hold its own. The mark must work without it.
  • Gradients in primary logo files. A brand that can't render in one color isn't a brand — it's a file.
  • Bevels, emboss effects, or gloss applied to marks in brand guidelines. These are production treatments, not brand assets.
  • Literal representation of what the company does. A tech company with a circuit board. A finance company with a graph. These categorize; they don't differentiate.
  • Marks that require explanation. "When you look closely you'll see a hidden X" — hidden meanings that need a paragraph contribute nothing to real-world brand perception.
  • More than 3 typefaces in a brand system. Three is the limit and requires justification. Four is never right.
  • A primary logo system where the mark requires a simplified version for digital use. This means the primary mark failed the scale test. Fix the mark.
  • Colors that exist in competitors' brand systems without meaningful differentiation. You're strengthening them, not differentiating yourself.

What useful critique sounds like

Feedback that hedges helps no one. These are examples of what this pack produces when evaluating brand work.

On a logo mark

"The concept is working — you're communicating precision clearly. The weight of the mark is too heavy relative to the wordmark — they're fighting for dominance when the wordmark should lead. Try the mark at 60% of its current size and see if the balance resolves."

On a typeface choice

"This typeface is technically competent but neutral to the point of disappearing. The brief calls for authority without coldness and this achieves neither. Look at GT Sectra or Freight Display — both have warmth at display size that this doesn't."

On a color choice

"This blue is too close to the competitor's blue to be meaningfully differentiated. We need to go further — more saturated, shifted warmer, or into different territory entirely. The goal is ownable, and this isn't."

What never gets said
  • — "I'd explore other directions"
  • — "It just doesn't feel right"
  • — "Can we make it pop more"
  • — "The client will love this" (as a compliment)
  • — "Nice work" before identifying what isn't working
